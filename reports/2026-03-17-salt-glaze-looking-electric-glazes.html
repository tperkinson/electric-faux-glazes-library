Salt-Glaze-Looking Electric Glazes: First Hunt

Mapped the first salt-glaze-effect research pass into electric-friendly DIY sodium treatments and commercial salt-track products.

2026-03-17 research, salt-glaze-look, electric, commercial, diy

Overview

This hunt split quickly into two very different categories:

  1. DIY systems that push sodium to the surface through soda ash, soda spray, or soda-heavy shino chemistry.
  2. Commercial products that sell a salt or soda-adjacent visual language without reproducing actual vapor glazing.

The main lesson is that electric faux salt work is less about copying historical salt exactly and more about deciding where the sodium event happens:

  • inside the glaze recipe
  • on top of the glaze surface
  • or only as a visual metaphor in a commercial product

Strong DIY Directions

Bates Clear plus soda ash is a surface-event recipe

Bates Clear With Soda Ash Sprinkle matters because it puts the sodium after glazing rather than only in the bucket.

  • The base glaze is stable enough to function as a carrier.
  • The sprinkled soda ash creates localized runs and disruption.
  • This is one of the cleaner routes toward selective salt/soda drama in a cone 6 oxidation workflow.

Visual Arts Center Shino is the chemistry-heavy route

Visual Arts Center Shino is the strongest actual recipe lead in this pass.

  • It already contains 9.09% soda ash.
  • The article explicitly frames soda ash as the driver for soda-salt crystals and related surface effects.
  • The electric cone 6 examples use crawling and an iron-sprayed overlay to emulate more atmospheric shino behavior.

This is likely the most defensible DIY bridge between electric oxidation and salt/soda-adjacent surface language.

Soda spray belongs in the same family

Minori Thorpe's Soda Spray (Oxidation) post is not a full glaze recipe, but it is an important process source.

  • Equal weights of soda ash and baking soda are sprayed onto glazed ware before cone 6 electric firing.
  • The before-and-after images show the sodium treatment doing real visual work.
  • It suggests that some salt-look testing should happen as glaze-plus-surface-treatment, not only as glaze formulation.

Strong Commercial Directions

Best immediate cone 6 commercial candidates

  • Mayco SW-118 Sea Salt gives the clearest textured accretion read through sage-green crystal blooms and a foamy matte surface.
  • Mayco SW-187 Himalayan Salt gives the warmer branch: pink and burnt-orange mottling, clear pooling, and a more flashed soda/salt palette.

These are not literal salt-glaze substitutes, but they are current, official, and easy to compare against the DIY sodium tests.

Laguna is real but under-described

Laguna WC-559 Artificial Salt is valuable mainly because it verifies that an explicitly named artificial-salt product is still live in an official catalog.

  • The current official page confirms the product and SKU.
  • It does not currently give enough descriptive copy to rank it against the Mayco options.
  • It probably belongs in a cone 10 branch unless the project intentionally widens beyond its present cone 6 center of gravity.

Best Next Test Grid

  • Bates Clear with two soda-ash loads: sparse sprinkle and aggressive sprinkle
  • Visual Arts Center Shino at one dip and two dips
  • Visual Arts Center Shino plus the sprayed iron branch
  • A neutral base glaze with Minori-style soda spray
  • Sea Salt straight and over Alabaster
  • Himalayan Salt on texture and on smoother forms

Risks To Watch

  • Sodium-on-surface methods can move fast from atmospheric to simply runny.
  • Controlled crawling reads rich; over-crawling reads like glaze failure.
  • Commercial salt-named products often evoke salt or soda visually without giving the orange-peel logic of true vapor glazing.
  • The strongest electric salt-look path may be layered process choreography rather than any single magic glaze.
