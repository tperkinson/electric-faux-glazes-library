Wood-Fire-Looking Electric Glazes: First Hunt

Logged the first DIY and commercial candidates for wood-fire-like electric surfaces, with recipes, product records, images, and process notes.

2026-03-17 research, wood-fire-look, electric, commercial, diy

Overview

This first hunt split the space into two tracks:

  1. DIY formulas and body additives that can be mixed and tuned.
  2. Commercial glaze lines and products that already push toward ash, shino, reduction, or slow-cool interaction effects in an electric kiln.

Strong DIY Directions

Directional ash and flashing

Ryan Coppage's electric wood-ash approach is important because it is not just a recipe. It is a process system:

  • spray the glaze directionally
  • mount ware on shells and wadding
  • let the setup create blocked paths, shell flashes, and runoff cues that read like side-firing

This is likely the most direct electric route toward wood-kiln body language.

Overlay stacks that mimic ash movement

The Hall-Hough cluster is valuable because it behaves like a stack:

  • V.C. Matte as the quiet base
  • Hannahs Fake Ash as a dry, mineral fake-ash overlay
  • Ashleighs Rivulet as the streaking layer
  • Frasca Ash as the actual wood-ash-heavy moving glaze

The project should treat this as a family rather than isolated recipes.

Body engineering matters too

Colored Shigaraki is a reminder that electric wood-fire emulation is not only about glaze chemistry. Feldspathic chips wedged into the body can change the read of the whole piece before glaze is even considered.

Strong Commercial Directions

Best broad commercial families

  • AMACO Potters Choice is explicit about reduction looks in oxidation and gives a wide layering matrix.
  • AMACO Shino is the clearest official commercial reduction-look-in-oxidation line in this first pass.
  • Mayco SW-122 Maycoshino is a strong thickness-responsive iron/shino commercial option.
  • Mayco SW218 Micro Ash looks promising for a more contemporary ash/crystalline charcoal surface.
  • Coyote Archies Base is valuable as a slow-cool-sensitive interaction glaze rather than a standalone finish.

Commercial vs DIY takeaway

Commercial glazes appear strongest when:

  • the goal is fast testing across many surfaces
  • layering behavior matters more than raw chemistry ownership
  • the user wants to compare multiple atmospheric looks quickly

DIY formulas remain strongest when:

  • the goal is to emulate actual ash chemistry or directional deposition
  • the user wants to tune clay-body response and flux balance
  • process theatrics like shells, wadding, drift, and controlled runoff are central

Best Next Test Grid

  • Flashing Wood Ash on a light cone 6 stoneware, sprayed from one direction
  • V.C. Matte under Hannahs Fake Ash
  • Green Dragon Matte under Frasca Ash and Ashleighs Rivulet
  • Maycoshino at one, two, and three coats
  • Micro Ash at two and three coats on texture
  • Archies Base layered over darker underglazes or high-contrast base colors

Risks To Watch

  • Nearly every promising faux-ash option in this first pass can run.
  • Ash chemistry is variable and should not be assumed stable across batches.
  • Slow cooling is likely a major lever for commercial interaction glazes.
  • A lot of the visual success comes from application choreography, not chemistry alone.
