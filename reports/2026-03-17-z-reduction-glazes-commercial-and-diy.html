Reduction-Look Glazes: Commercial and DIY Hunt

Added a reduction-focused batch of commercial products and DIY cone 6 recipes spanning copper red, shino, iron red, tenmoku, and oil-spot looks.

2026-03-17 research, reduction-look, electric, commercial, diy

Overview

This pass focused on the reduction side of faux-atmospheric electric glazing rather than the wood-ash side.

The batch breaks into four clear visual families:

  1. Copper reds and oxbloods
  2. Electric shinos
  3. Iron reds
  4. Dark tenmoku and oil-spot surfaces

Strong Commercial Finds

Copper red lane

  • Coyote Oxblood is the most literal commercial faux-reduction red in this pass because the manufacturer says it creates local reduction in an electric kiln.
  • AMACO PC-70 Copper Red is the most explicit oxidation-to-reduction bridge in the major-brand brush-on space because AMACO says it gives the same results in oxidation as a traditional reduction copper glaze.

Shino lane

  • Coyote Shino is a strong practical commercial shino because it is positioned as giving a gas-fired look in any kiln, while also being stable and not runny.
  • This makes it a useful commercial counterpart to more chemistry-driven DIY electric shino work.

Dark reduction lane

  • Mayco SW-111 Wrought Iron covers the oil-spot side of reduction aesthetics unusually well for cone 6 oxidation.
  • Its importance is not just color. It promises a dappled gloss-matte black surface with only two coats, which makes it one of the quickest commercial routes into dark reduction-like surfaces.

Strong DIY Finds

Electric shino system

  • Visual Arts Center Shino is the strongest DIY shino result in this hunt because it was explicitly designed for cone 6 electric firing.
  • The key insight is that the recipe is a base system. Thickness and optional iron additions are what shift it from white crawling shino toward orange flashing shino.

Iron red lane

  • Tomato Red Base is the more engineered red and should be tested when the goal is a richer glossy-satin reduction-red analog.
  • Ketchup Red is the simpler matte iron-red baseline and belongs in the same test series because it may deliver a more mineral, less polished result.

Tenmoku lane

  • Tenmoku Gold gives the archive a reduction-look path that depends on cooling rather than just chemistry or coat count.
  • It is especially valuable because it shifts the conversation away from reds and shinos into amber-black teadust territory.

Main Synthesis

Commercial reduction-look glazes are strongest when:

  • the target look is narrow and recognizable, like oxblood, shino, or oil spot
  • the user wants fast coat-count comparisons
  • the process needs to stay simple and repeatable

DIY reduction-look recipes are strongest when:

  • surface language depends on more than one lever
  • layering, spraying, or optional additions are part of the look
  • the user wants to tune a family of effects rather than buy a fixed result

Best Next Test Grid

  • Coyote Oxblood vs AMACO PC-70 Copper Red at thin, medium, and thick application
  • Visual Arts Center Shino base vs the 4% red-iron-oxide variant on both smooth and textured clay
  • Ketchup Red vs Tomato Red Base on the same iron-bearing and buff stoneware bodies
  • Tenmoku Gold slow cool vs normal cool
  • Mayco Wrought Iron on smooth, carved, and speckled clay bodies

Risks To Watch

  • Commercial copper reds still depend heavily on thickness, so “easy” does not mean foolproof.
  • Electric shino success depends on controlled crawling rather than only color.
  • Dark reduction-like surfaces may depend on cooling cycles as much as on recipe choice.
  • Several of the strongest faux-reduction surfaces in this batch can become less convincing if applied too evenly.
